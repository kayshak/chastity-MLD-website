What is MLD?

Understanding Manual Lymphatic Drainage.

A gentle, precise technique grounded in the anatomy of the lymphatic system — the body's network for clearing fluid, waste, and supporting immune function.

A clinical, nursing perspective

Manual Lymphatic Drainage (MLD) is a specialized, light-pressure technique that encourages the natural movement of lymph fluid through the body. Unlike deep-tissue massage, MLD works at the surface of the skin, following the directional pathways of the lymphatic system to gently guide fluid toward healthy drainage points.

Performed by a Registered Nurse, MLD is informed by a thorough understanding of anatomy, contraindications, and post-surgical considerations — ensuring the technique is applied safely and appropriately for each individual's condition.

Why anatomy & safety matter

The lymphatic system is intricate, and proper technique requires knowing where — and where not — to apply pressure. A clinical background means swelling, inflammation, and recovery markers are assessed with a trained eye, and care is adjusted accordingly.

  • Supports reduction of swelling and fluid retention.
  • Encourages circulation and the body's natural healing response.
  • Applied with clinical awareness of contraindications and recovery stage.

A note on safety: MLD is a complement to — not a replacement for — the guidance of your surgeon or physician. Care is always coordinated with your broader medical plan.
